I grew up rich. Not yacht rich, but the kind where your parents tell you they're "comfortable". I went to private schools from K-12 and didn't have to take out loans for college. All of this is to say that when I cast aspersions on high-paying tech jobs, keep the above in mind because I've been extremely lucky to be sheltered from some financial pressure on my career decisions.

A major selling point of Harvey Mudd's marketing is a focus on the intersection of technology and society. In my experience, the degree program seems structured to incentivize shying away from taking a critical look at society – at least from some perspectives. 

In addition to a pretty robust technical workload, we're required to take 10 humanities classes to graduate. 4 must be in the same discipline. However, the difficulty of our technical program means that the path of least resistance for most people is to take easy humanities classes that overlap with our technical education and therefore require the least amount of [context switching](https://www.apa.org/topics/research/multitasking) necessary. I gave into this pressure. My concentration, linguistics, sees a huge overlap with formal computer science, which made my semesters significantly easier.
